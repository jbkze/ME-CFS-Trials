Abstract
Background‚Äā Myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome (ME/CFS) is a debilitating multisystem 
disease characterized by heterogeneous symptom patterns. Previous work suggested that specific symptoms 
tend to co‚ÄĎoccur, pointing toward underlying biological mechanisms. This study aimed to empirically validate 
literature‚ÄĎbased, hypothesis‚ÄĎdriven symptom clusters and assess whether they reflect distinct neuroimmune and 
autonomic pathophysiological pathways.
Methods‚Äā Symptom data from 748 adults with ME/CFS (‚Č•20‚ÄČyears) participating in the APAV‚ÄĎME/CFS study were 
analyzed. Symptoms were assigned to predefined mechanistic groups informed by current pathophysiological 
hypotheses. Exploratory and Confirmatory Factor Analyses, followed by Structural Equation Modeling (SEM), evaluated 
the coherence, distinctiveness, and hierarchical structure of each cluster. Robustness was tested using a stratified, 
randomized training dataset.
Results‚Äā A coherent Brain factor (brain fog, sensory hypersensitivity, visual disturbances, sleep disturbances, 
headaches) showed excellent fit (RMSEA‚ÄČ=‚ÄČ0.021; CFI‚ÄČ=‚ÄČ0.996). Gastrointestinal symptoms demonstrated stronger 
internal consistency than Immune symptoms, and model comparisons supported a two‚ÄĎfactor Gut‚ÄďImmune structure. 
Across all analyses, symptom groups emerged as internally consistent and statistically distinct. A higher‚ÄĎorder SEM 
including a common latent factor yielded excellent fit for the Autonomic symptom complex.
Conclusions‚Äā The findings support ME/CFS as a complex neuroimmune‚Äďautonomic multisystem disorder and 
suggest that symptom clusters align with functional biological systems. Mechanism-aligned symptom subgrouping 
may enable pathophysiology-guided diagnostics, patient stratification, and targeted therapeutic development. The 
proposed interpretations of underlying mechanisms derive from the integration of existing literature and were not 
directly measured in this study. The identified clusters therefore indicate mechanistic alignment rather than direct 
mechanistic validation.

Introduction
Myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome 
(ME/CFS; ICD‚ÄĎ10‚ÄĎGM‚ÄĎ2025: G93.3) is a debilitating 
multisystem disease characterized by a wide range of 
symptoms [1] and substantial impairment in daily func¬≠
tioning. In most cases, ME/CFS follows an infectious 
trigger and is associated with long‚ÄĎlasting disability and 
markedly reduced quality of life [2]. Current evidence 
indicates disturbances across multiple regulatory sys¬≠
tems‚ÄĒincluding the nervous, endocrine, and immune 
systems‚ÄĒas well as impaired cellular energy metabolism 
and chronic low‚ÄĎgrade inflammation, suggesting a break¬≠
down of physiological homeostasis in several intercon¬≠
nected biological networks [3‚Äď7].
A hallmark feature of ME/CFS is post‚ÄĎexertional mal¬≠
aise (PEM), a delayed and prolonged exacerbation of 
symptoms following minimal physical or cognitive exer¬≠
tion. PEM is accompanied by profound reductions in 
physical and mental energy, rapid deterioration of overall 
functioning, and a broad array of fluctuating symptoms, 
including neurocognitive impairment, immunological 
disturbances, sleep dysfunction, and autonomic dysregu¬≠
lation [5].
Despite the multisystemic nature of ME/CFS, little is 
known about how symptoms cluster interact, or evolve 
over the course of the disease. In a previous exploratory 
study (Study 1), we identified preliminary patterns sug¬≠
gesting that certain symptoms co‚ÄĎoccur more frequently 
than expected, pointing toward underlying mechanistic 
relationships [8]. These findings motivated the present 
study (Study 2), which aims to empirically test and vali¬≠
date these hypothesized symptom clusters using Explor¬≠
atory and Confirmatory Factor Analyses and Structural 
Equation Modeling.
We hypothesized that distinct symptom groups would 
emerge that align with known pathophysiological path¬≠
ways‚ÄĒparticularly neuroimmune, autonomic, gastro¬≠
intestinal, and central nervous system mechanisms. 
Identifying such mechanism‚ÄĎaligned symptom clusters 
may support more precise diagnostics, inform stratifica¬≠
tion strategies, and guide the development of targeted 
therapeutic approaches. Building on these findings, the 
subsequent phases of this research program (Studies 3‚Äď5; 
see Fig. 1) will investigate potential modifiers of symp¬≠
tom architecture, with a particular focus on sex and ill¬≠
ness duration. Studies 3 and 4 will examine possible 
sex-related differences in the Brain/Brain2 and Auto¬≠
nomic clusters (Study 3) as well as in the Gut/Immune/
Immune2 clusters (Study 4), relating these patterns to 
established pathophysiological processes in ME/CFS and 
contributing to the development of an integrated patho¬≠
physiological model. Study 5 will investigate potential 
changes in ME/CFS symptom clusters over the course of 
the disease, aiming to classify these changes pathophysi¬≠
ologically while differentiating between sexes.
Methods
This study is based on data from the APAV-ME/CFS 
study. The predominantly qualitative APAV-ME/CFS 
study focused primarily on the doctor-patient relation¬≠
ship in ME/CFS from the perspective of patients and 
their relatives. In addition, a range of basic data on gen¬≠
der/sex, age, etc., as well as information on whether the 
participant has a medical diagnosis of ME/CFS was also 
collected (see below).
Sampling
A total of 1,238 people (ME/CFS patients and their close 
relatives) took part in the APAV-ME/CFS study. They 
were recruited through voluntary participation and 
snowball sampling. To this end, contact was made in 
summer 2022 with six ME/CFS patient organizations in 
Germany as well as the Fatigue Centre at Charit√© in Ber¬≠
lin and the Chronic Fatigue Centre for Young People at 
the Technical University of Munich. The organizations 
forwarded the call for study participation to ME/CFS 
patients and their relatives. Through them, the infor¬≠
mation reached other affected persons. Long-term or 
post-COVID patients who did not meet the Canadian or 
International Consensus Criteria for ME/CFS were not 
included in the study.
The recruitment strategy via patient organizations and 
snowball sampling may introduce selection bias. How¬≠
ever, this approach is widely used in ME/CFS research 
due to the absence of population-based registries and the 
limited availability of clinically diagnosed cohorts.

international epidemiological data reporting a female 
predominance of 70‚Äď80%. Importantly, the aim of this 
study was not to estimate population prevalence but to 
examine the internal structure of symptom clusters. 
Data from participating children and adolescents (up to 
20‚ÄČyears of age), relatives of ME/CFS patients, and the

Data analysis
All reported symptoms were coded as dichotomous vari¬≠
ables (presence vs. absence). Dichotomizing symptoms 
into yes/no categories reduces variance and can attenuate 
factor loadings, internal consistency (Cronbach‚Äôs őĪ), and 
SEM fit indices, particularly in heterogeneous domains 
such as the Immune and Autonomic clusters. This meth¬≠
odological constraint likely contributed to the moderate 
loadings observed in these domains. Nevertheless, global 
model fit indices remained excellent, indicating that the 
underlying latent structure was robust despite reduced 
measurement sensitivity. In the subsequent statistical 
analysis of the symptoms regularly occurring in the par¬≠
ticipants - except PEM and fatigue, as these are the core 
diagnostic criteria for an ME/CFS diagnosis and must 
therefore be present in all patients - tests for bivariate 
associations were first conducted, including a check of 
internal consistency (cross-tabulations, Cram√©r‚Äôs V and 
tetrachoric correlations). The Exploratory Factor Analysis 
(EFA) and Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA) were then 
used to examine whether the previously defined, hypoth¬≠

could be identified as statistically detectable clusters.
All analyses were based on the sample of 748 patients, 
providing a sufficient number of cases for exploratory and 
confirmatory factor analyses. Although it is methodolog¬≠

samples, we opted for a combined analysis because the 
sample size is too small to be split. To assess the robust¬≠

Discussion
The present study represents the second phase of a 
multi‚ÄĎstage research program aimed at elucidating the 
symptom architecture of ME/CFS. Building on prelimi¬≠
nary findings from Study 1, which suggested sex‚ÄĎspecific 
patterns and co‚ÄĎoccurrence of symptoms [8], this study 
sought to empirically validate predefined, hypothe¬≠
sis‚ÄĎdriven symptom clusters using factor analytic meth¬≠
ods and structural equation modeling (SEM). Across a 
large cohort of clinically diagnosed ME/CFS patients, we 
identified four robust and internally coherent symptom 
domains‚ÄĒBrain, Immune, Autonomic, and Gut‚ÄĒthat 
align closely with current pathophysiological hypotheses. 
Replication in a stratified, randomized training data¬≠
set confirmed the stability of these clusters, underscor¬≠
ing their structural validity. It is important to emphasize 
that the biological mechanisms discussed here were not 
directly assessed in this study. Mechanistic interpreta¬≠
tions are inferred from converging evidence in the litera¬≠
ture and should be understood as alignment rather than 
empirical validation.
Symptom clusters reflect distinct neuroimmune and 
autonomic mechanisms
Brain cluster
The Brain cluster, comprising neurocognitive, sensory, 
and sleep‚ÄĎrelated symptoms, is consistent with extensive 
evidence of central nervous system abnormalities in ME/
CFS. Neuroimaging studies have demonstrated struc¬≠
tural and functional alterations in the brainstem, thala¬≠
mus, and prefrontal cortex [9‚Äď14], including reduced 
cerebral blood flow [15], neuroglial dysfunction [13], and 
widespread neuroinflammation [14]. Altered sleep archi¬≠
tecture has also been documented [16]. These findings 
support the interpretation of the Brain cluster as a mani¬≠
festation of network‚ÄĎlevel dysregulation within thal¬≠
amo‚ÄĎcortical and brainstem circuits.
Immune cluster
The Immune cluster aligns with well‚ÄĎestablished immu¬≠
nological abnormalities in ME/CFS, including impaired 
NK‚ÄĎcell function [17], genetically inferred immune dys¬≠
regulation [18], chronic activation of pro‚ÄĎinflammatory 
cytokines [18, 19], and reactivation of latent herpesvi¬≠
ruses such as HHV‚ÄĎ6 [20] and EBV [21]. These processes 
contribute to sickness‚ÄĎbehavior‚ÄĎlike symptoms [22], 
neuroinflammation [4, 23], and increased susceptibil¬≠
ity to infections. The coherence of this cluster reinforces 
the role of immune dysregulation as a central biological 
driver of symptom expression.
Autonomic cluster
The Autonomic cluster reflects autonomic dysfunction 
across cardiovascular, respiratory, and thermoregula¬≠
tory domains. Reduced heart rate variability, orthostatic 
intolerance, tachycardia, and chronotropic incompetence 
have been repeatedly documented [24, 25]. Dysregula¬≠
tion of vagal pathways may contribute to nausea and 
delayed gastric emptying [26], while altered chemorecep¬≠
tor and baroreceptor function may influence respiratory 
rate, CO‚āā regulation, and cerebral perfusion [15]. Ther¬≠
moregulatory instability and vascular tone dysregulation 
have also been reported [27]. These findings support the 
conceptualization of ME/CFS as a disorder of integrated 
neuroimmune‚Äďautonomic regulation [28].

Gut cluster
The Gut cluster captures gastrointestinal symptoms 
that extend beyond autonomic dysregulation and reflect 
distinct enteric pathomechanisms. Dysbiosis, reduced 
microbial diversity, and altered microbiota composition 
have been documented in ME/CFS [29, 30], alongside 
increased intestinal permeability and bacterial transloca¬≠

pathways through the gut‚Äďbrain‚Äďimmune axis [33, 34]. 
The separation of Gut and Immune clusters, despite 
their functional interconnections, highlights the pres¬≠

subsystems.
Analyses conducted on the stratified, randomized 
training dataset yielded directionally consistent factor 
loadings and comparable fit indices to the full dataset. 
No structural deviations were observed, supporting the 
robustness of the identified clusters. Detailed results are 
available in the additional online media.
Overall model reflecting distinct neuroimmune and 
autonomic mechanisms

correspond to functional body systems and known bio¬≠
logical pathways. The presence of ‚Äúbridging symptoms‚ÄĚ

Comparison with previous cluster and factor analytic 
studies
Our results are broadly consistent with earlier cluster 
and factor analyses, which also identified neurocogni¬≠
tive, autonomic, and immune‚ÄĎrelated symptom domains 
[6, 35‚Äď39]. However, the present study extends previ¬≠
ous work in several important ways. First, we tested 
predefined mechanistic clusters derived from patho¬≠
physiological hypotheses rather than relying solely on 
data‚ÄĎdriven approaches. Second, our sample is among 
the largest analyzed to date in ME/CFS cluster research. 
Third, replication in a stratified dataset strengthens the 
robustness and generalizability of the findings. The emer¬≠
gence of a distinct Gut cluster and the identification of 
bridging phenomena provide additional granularity that 
has been underrepresented in earlier studies.
Implications for diagnostic refinement and translational 
research
The identified clusters have direct implications for diag¬≠
nostic practice and translational research. Current diag¬≠
nostic instruments, including CCC‚ÄĎbased questionnaires 
[40] and widely used clinical tools in Germany [41], do 
not fully capture the biological architecture of ME/CFS. 
Several relevant symptoms‚ÄĒsuch as visual disturbances, 
muscle weakness, and gastrointestinal or respiratory 
bridging symptoms‚ÄĒare insufficiently represented or 
absent. Our findings suggest that diagnostic tools should 
incorporate mechanism‚ÄĎaligned subscales reflecting neu¬≠
rocognitive, immune, autonomic, and enteric domains. 
Such differentiation may improve diagnostic precision, 
support patient stratification, and guide individualized 
therapeutic strategies.
From a translational perspective, the clusters provide 
testable mechanistic hypotheses for biomarker develop¬≠
ment. Integrating symptom clusters with immune profil¬≠
ing [3, 17, 18], microbiome analyses [30‚Äď33], metabolic 
markers, and neuroimaging [9‚Äď15] could refine pheno¬≠
type definitions and identify biological signatures associ¬≠
ated with specific symptom domains. This approach may 
facilitate the development of targeted interventions and 
improve the design of clinical trials by enabling more 
homogeneous subgroup selection.
However, the diagnostic and stratification implica¬≠
tions outlined here should be understood as conceptual 
and hypothesis-generating. Clinical implementation will 
require validation with biological markers, longitudinal 
data, and independent cohorts.
Recent biomarker studies have demonstrated immune 
activation and metabolic abnormalities not explicable by 
inactivity [3], while deep phenotyping studies have high¬≠
lighted multisystem involvement [1]. Qualitative research 
further indicates that patients consistently describe bio¬≠
logical triggers‚ÄĒmost commonly viral infections‚ÄĒand 
report frequent misdiagnosis in psychosomatic contexts 
[42, 43]. The present findings thus contribute to the 
growing body of evidence supporting a biological disease 
model.
Methodological considerations and future directions
Despite the excellent fit indices of our SEM models, sev¬≠
eral methodological limitations warrant consideration. 
Dichotomous symptom coding reduces variance and 
may obscure gradations in symptom severity. Measure¬≠
ment invariance across sex, age, and illness duration has 
not yet been assessed. Moderate Cronbach‚Äôs őĪ values are 
therefore not unexpected in multisystem conditions such 
as ME/CFS, where symptom domains are inherently het¬≠
erogeneous. In this context, moderate internal consis¬≠
tency does not weaken the construct‚Äôs validity, especially 
when it is supported by converging evidence from EFA, 
CFA, and SEM.
Differences in explained variance between EFA and 
CFA are expected because CFA applies a more con¬≠
strained structure, which typically increases the pro¬≠
portion of variance attributed to the latent factor. These 
differences do not indicate inconsistency but reflect the 
distinct purposes of exploratory versus confirmatory 
modeling. Importantly, the biological mechanisms dis¬≠
cussed in this manuscript were not directly measured. 
They represent literature-based interpretations aligned 
with the observed symptom structure rather than empiri¬≠
cal validation.
Future studies should employ polytomous symptom 
scales, incorporate invariance testing, and validate the 
identified clusters in larger, independent, and ideally 
longitudinal cohorts. Epidemiological studies indicate 
age‚ÄĎrelated incidence peaks [44] and substantial disease 
burden [2], underscoring the need for refined phenotyp¬≠
ing across demographic subgroups. The next phases of 
this research program will therefore examine how symp¬≠
tom clusters vary by sex, age, and disease duration and 
explore their associations with biological markers. Such 
work will be essential for advancing precision-medicine 
approaches and developing targeted preventive and ther¬≠
apeutic strategies.
Limitations
Participants were recruited via ME/CFS self-help groups 
and the two German treatment centers in Berlin and 
Munich. As recruitment was based on self-selection and 
snowballing, selection and homogeneity effects may have 
occurred, but with a sample size of n‚ÄČ=‚ÄČ748 these can be 
considered moderate. A comparison with the actual gen¬≠
der- and age-related prevalence/incidence in Germany 
was not possible due to a lack of reliable data. Women 
are likely slightly overrepresented (611 women vs. 138 
men), but prevalence and incidence peaks correspond to 

Page 14 of 16
Habermann-Horstmeier and Horstmeier Journal of Translational Medicine          (2026) 24:606 
international findings (incidence peak: 30‚Äď39‚ÄČyears; prev¬≠
alence peak: 40‚Äď59‚ÄČyears) [44, 45].
Participants‚Äô medical diagnoses and symptoms were 
self‚ÄĎreported and did not rely on clinical verification. This 
may introduce selection and diagnostic bias. However, 
the aim of the study was not to estimate prevalence or 
evaluate diagnostic accuracy but to examine the internal 
structure of symptom clusters. Factor-analytic methods 
are comparatively robust to such sampling heterogeneity.
Self-report Data may also be affected by recall bias. In 
addition, the pathophysiological classifications discussed 
in this study are derived from existing literature, rather 
than from direct biological measurements. Given the 
limited sample size, exploratory and confirmatory fac¬≠
tor analyses (EFA and CFA) could not be conducted on 
independent subsamples; instead, the full dataset was 
used. To assess the robustness of the findings, additional 
analyses were performed using a randomized, stratified 
training subsample stratified by sex and disease duration. 
While this procedure improves representativeness and 
reduces the risk of systematic bias, it does not replace 
validation in fully independent samples.
The dichotomization of symptoms (yes/no) further 
constitutes a methodological constraint, as binary indi¬≠
cators reduces reduce variability and statistical power in 
structural equation modeling (SEM) and may attenuate 
factor loadings. This likely contributed to the moderate 
loadings observed in domains such as the Brain cluster. 
Dichotomous items are nevertheless common in clinical 
epidemiology because they reduce respondent burden 
and minimize recall bias. However, they capture sever¬≠
ity gradients only indirectly. Despite this limitation, all 
CFA and SEM models demonstrated excellent global fit 
indices, indicating that the underlying latent structure 
is robust. Future phases of this research program will 
therefore employ polytomous symptom scales to capture 
severity more precisely and further strengthen measure¬≠
ment sensitivity. Finally, potential effects of sex, age, or 
disease duration on the latent factors were not exam¬≠
ined in the present analyses. Likewise, formal tests of 
measurement invariance across demographic subgroups 
were not feasible. These aspects will be addressed in later 
phases of the research program.
The use of maximum likelihood (ML) estimation with 
binary indicators represents a methodological limita¬≠
tion. Although ML performs adequately in large samples, 
categorical estimators such as WLSMV are generally 
preferable for dichotomous data and will therefore be 
implemented in future analyses. This limitation does not 
substantially affect the overall interpretation of the latent 
structure but should be addressed in later phases of the 
research program.
In addition, we were unable to elegantly map local 
dependencies of certain symptoms (e.g. heart ‚ÜĒ gut) 
using the Stata 12 software available to us. As a result, 
parameters could be distorted or fit indices misleading. 
Multiple model comparisons risked alpha inflation. Thus, 
Bonferroni correction was applied. Data on psychiatric 
comorbidities, medication use, and socioeconomic sta¬≠
tus were not available in the APAV-ME/CFS dataset and 
could therefore not be included as covariates. Conse¬≠
quently, potential confounding factors could not be con¬≠
trolled, which may have influenced symptom reporting 
and biased the identified symptom clusters. Moreover, 
recorded psychiatric diagnoses may themselves represent 
misdiagnoses [42], further complicating the interpreta¬≠
tion of these effects. Although these limitations must be 
acknowledged, the primary focus of this study was on 
the internal structure of symptom clusters rather than on 
causal inference, and factor-analytic methods are com¬≠
paratively robust to such confounding influences. Future 
studies will incorporate these variables to allow more 
comprehensive modeling and to further refine the inter¬≠
pretation of symptom domains.
Conclusions
Exploratory and Confirmatory Factor Analyses, together 
with Structural Equation Modeling (SEM), consistently 
revealed distinct, internally coherent symptom clusters 
in ME/CFS that demonstrate excellent model fit and 
align with current neuroimmune and autonomic patho¬≠
physiological hypotheses. These findings strengthen the 
conceptualization of ME/CFS as a complex multisys¬≠
tem disorder and provide translationally relevant evi¬≠
dence that the symptom patterns observed here reflect 
the underlying biological mechanisms. The statistically 
validated clusters correspond closely to previous obser¬≠
vations and provide a basis for mechanism-informed 
diagnostics and individualized therapeutic strategies. 
However, although the identified clusters align with the 
aforementioned neuroimmune and autonomic mecha¬≠
nisms, they do not directly validate them, as the bio¬≠
logical signaling pathways were derived from previous 
research and were not measured in the present dataset. 
Furthermore, the Gut and Immune factors were defined 
by only two indicators, resulting in perfectly identified 
models that do not permit evaluation of global fit. This 
limits the reliability of these constructs and should be 
addressed in future work by expanding the item sets.
Our results also highlight the need for diagnostic 
instruments that more accurately reflect the biological 
architecture of ME/CFS. Future questionnaires should 
incorporate differentiated subscales‚ÄĒsuch as gastro¬≠
intestinal, immune, neurocognitive, and autonomic 
domains‚ÄĒto better capture disease heterogeneity and 
guide targeted interventions. Additional symptom 
domains, including muscle and ocular symptoms, war¬≠
rant inclusion as separate subscales.

Further research should replicate these clusters in 
larger, independent, and ideally longitudinal cohorts, 
while integrating biological markers such as immune 
signatures, microbiome profiles, metabolic parameters, 
and neuroimaging findings to refine mechanistic under¬≠
standing. Analytic approaches should also be expanded 
to include machine-learning approaches (e.g., clustering 
algorithms, random forests, or network-based commu¬≠
nity detection) to complement factor-analytic techniques 
and explore potential nonlinear or higher-order symp¬≠
tom structures.
Despite the excellent fit indices of our SEM mod¬≠
els, methodological limitations must be acknowledged. 
Dichotomous symptom coding reduces variance and 
may obscure subtle effects, and measurement invari¬≠
ance across sex, age, and illness duration has not yet been 
assessed. Future studies should therefore employ polyto¬≠

to enhance robustness and generalizability. In addition, 
examining symptom clusters across demographic and 
clinical subgroups may support the development of tai¬≠
lored preventive and therapeutic approaches.
