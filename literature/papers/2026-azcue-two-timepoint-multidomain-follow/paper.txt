Abstract
Background  Post-COVID condition (PCC) and Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (ME/CFS) show 
marked clinical overlap, suggesting a shared post-infectious pathophysiology. This study aims to characterize the 
longitudinal change of autonomic function, small-fiber integrity, cognitive performance, and clinical symptoms in 
PCC and ME/CFS, and to determine whether trajectories differ between diagnostic groups.
Methods  Thirty-eight participants (21 PCC, 17 ME/CFS) underwent two standardized evaluations separated by 
a median of 31 months. Assessments included comprehensive autonomic testing, small-fiber evaluation, and an 
extensive neuropsychological battery.
Results  ME/CFS showed longer disease duration than PCC at baseline (median 42 vs. 12 months), while the interval 
between evaluations was comparable (31 vs. 30 months). Baseline profiles were largely overlapping, although ME/CFS 
showed nominally higher QST warm detection thresholds (p = 0.034), greater autonomic symptom burden (p = 0.038), 
and lower hemodynamic scores (p = 0.019), none surviving FDR correction. Cross-domain analyses linked small-
fiber symptoms with autonomic symptom burden (Rho = 0.65, pFDR = 0.002) and fatigue (Rho = 0.55, pFDR = 0.018), 
while fatigue was negatively associated with processing speed (Rho = − 0.57, pFDR = 0.004), attention (Rho = − 0.49, 
pFDR = 0.018), and executive function (Rho = − 0.44, pFDR = 0.047). Rank-transformed mixed-effects models identified 
FDR-corrected Time effects, with increases in CHEPs (pFDR < 0.001) and verbal memory (pFDR = 0.010), and decreases 
in processing speed (pFDR = 0.006) and QST cold thresholds (pFDR = 0.038).
Conclusions  PCC and ME/CFS showed broadly overlapping multidomain profiles, with particularly similar profiles at 
follow-up. This suggests that, among individuals with persistent symptoms, PCC may increasingly resemble longer-

​Introduction
Longitudinal evidence shows a substantial clinical and 
biological overlap between post-COVID condition 
(PCC, or long COVID) and Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/
Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (ME/CFS), both character­
ized by long-lasting fatigue, post-exertional malaise, cog­
nitive dysfunction, and markedly reduced quality of life 
[1–5]. These overlapping post-infectious syndromes are 
increasingly recognized as part of a shared spectrum of 
chronic, multisystemic illness involving immune dysreg­
ulation, neuroinflammation, and autonomic nervous sys­
tem (ANS) imbalance [6–12].
Longitudinal studies have demonstrated that approxi­
mately 50% of individuals recovering from SARS-CoV-2 
infection meet diagnostic criteria for this syndrome for at 
least 12 months [13, 14]. Fatigue, post-exertional malaise, 
cognitive impairment, and unrefreshing sleep remain 
among the most frequent and disabling complaints, with 
up to 43% of these patients fulfilling ME/CFS diagnostic 
criteria [1–3]. This symptom overlap complicates differ­
ential diagnosis and underscores the need for objective 
physiological and neuropsychological characterization [1, 
15].
The temporal course of both PCC and ME/CFS is typi­
cally chronic, fluctuating, and heterogeneous. Although 
some symptoms, such as fatigue or sleep disturbance, 
may gradually decline over time, cognitive dysfunction 
often persists or even becomes more pronounced [16–
18]. Most patients experience a relapsing–remitting tra­
jectory rather than sustained recovery, with significant 
impact on daily functioning, social participation, and 
employment [1, 19, 20].
Dysautonomia has emerged as a major contributor to 
the clinical burden in both conditions. Between 27% and 
66% of individuals with PCC present moderate to severe 
autonomic dysfunction, manifesting as orthostatic intol­
erance, tachycardia, dizziness, or blood pressure insta­
bility, often resembling postural orthostatic tachycardia 
syndrome (POTS) [11, 21–23]. In ME/CFS, chronic auto­
nomic dysfunction affects up to 90% of patients and is 
frequently characterized by sympathetic predominance 
and impaired baroreflex control, correlating with greater 
fatigue severity and cognitive difficulties [16, 24–26]. 
Neuroimaging and biomarker studies support a neuroim­
mune basis for these abnormalities, revealing brainstem 
and white matter changes, endothelial dysfunction, and 
autoantibodies against adrenergic and muscarinic recep­
tors [9, 27–29].
The long-term impact of these autonomic and cogni­
tive disturbances is profound, often leading to sustained 
disability, emotional distress, and reduced quality of life 
[24, 30, 31]. While some longitudinal studies describe 
partial recovery, a significant subset of patients contin­
ues to experience persistent or relapsing symptoms for 
years [32–35]. Understanding the trajectory and inter­
play between cognitive and autonomic dysfunction is 
essential to clarify shared mechanisms and guide clinical 
management.
In this context, the present study aimed to investi­
gate changes in cognitive, autonomic, and small-fiber 
abnormalities between two-timepoints in PCC and ME/
CFS, and to determine whether these changes differed 
between diagnostic groups.
Materials and methods
Participants and demographic data
All participants underwent a comprehensive clinical 
evaluation, including the collection of demographic and 
clinical data such as age, sex, years of education, disease 
duration, and other relevant variables. The study cohort 
comprised 38 individuals, 21 patients diagnosed with 
PCC and 17 with ME/CFS, who were prospectively eval­
uated over time for autonomic, cognitive, and small-fiber 
involvement under standardized diagnostic and ethical 
procedures.
The inclusion criteria encompassed adults aged 
between 18 and 70 years with adequate comprehension 
and communication abilities. Exclusion criteria included 
pregnancy or lactation, severe trauma, alcoholism, drug 
addiction, severe cardiac disease, radiological evidence of 
structural brain pathology, concomitant conditions that 
could influence study results, and the use of immuno­
modulatory treatments.
Participants were assigned to the PCC or ME/CFS 
cohorts according to the primary clinical diagnosis and 
illness onset recorded at recruitment. PCC participants 
met the NICE definition for post-COVID condition, with 
documented or clinically supported SARS-CoV-2 infec­
tion preceding symptom onset. Acute infection was con­
firmed through a positive nasal PCR, the detection of IgG 
and/or IgM antibodies against SARS-CoV-2, or a medi­
cal report supporting the diagnosis. Exclusion criteria 
specific to this group included respiratory disease lasting 
beyond 12 weeks, admission to an intensive care unit, or 
severe bilateral pneumonia requiring hospitalization.

ME/CFS participants had received a clinical diagno­
sis of ME/CFS according to Fukuda criteria [36] before 
inclusion in the present study. In this cohort, ME/CFS 
symptoms preceded any subsequent SARS-CoV-2 infec­
tion when present; therefore, participants were not clas­
sified as post-COVID-onset ME/CFS. Some ME/CFS 
participants may have experienced SARS-CoV-2 infec­
tion during the follow-up period, potentially worsening 
pre-existing symptoms, but their primary diagnosis and 
illness onset were not attributable to COVID-19.
Thus, the two cohorts were not assumed to be mutually 
exclusive at the symptom-criterion level. Instead, they 
were defined according to clinical recruitment diagnosis 
and illness onset: post-COVID-onset chronic illness ver­
sus established ME/CFS.
The study protocol was approved by the Basque Drug 
Research Ethics Committee [Comité de Ética de la Inves­
tigación con medicamentos de Euskadi (CEIm-E), refer­
ence PI2020210], and all participants provided written 
informed consent in accordance with the Declaration of 
Helsinki.
Autonomic function assessment
Integrity of the ANS was evaluated through a multi­
modal battery assessing sudomotor, cardiovascular, and 
orthostatic domains. Sudomotor function, reflecting 
sympathetic cholinergic activity, was measured with the 
Sudoscan® device (Impeto Medica© Paris, France) which 
quantifies electrochemical skin conductance (ESC, µS) at 
the palms and soles. Cardiovascular autonomic control 
was examined using the Task Force Monitor® (CNSys­
tems© Graz, Austria) under standardized conditions. 
Frequency-domain spectral analysis provided heart rate 
variability (HRV) and blood pressure variability (BPV) 
indices: high-frequency normalized units (HFnu) were 
taken as a marker of parasympathetic tone, while low-fre­
quency normalized units (LFnu) of diastolic BP reflected 
sympathetic vasomotor modulation. Baroreflex sensi­
tivity (BRS) was computed using the sequence method. 
Cardiovagal (parasympathetic) reactivity was further 
evaluated through the deep-breathing test. The Valsalva 
maneuver (40 mmHg for 15 s) enabled the assessment of 
combined vagal and sympathetic responses, including the 
Valsalva ratio and systolic pressure recovery time (PRT). 
Orthostatic responses were assessed via passive head-up 
tilt at 60° for at least ten minutes with continuous cardio­
vascular monitoring to detect orthostatic hypotension, 
POTS, or autonomic failure. All assessments were per­
formed by trained neurologists following standardized 
protocols to ensure reliability.
Small-fiber function assessment
Thermal and pain small-fiber functions were assessed 
using the TSA-2 system (Medoc Advanced Medical 
System©, Ramat Yishai, Israel), including Contact Heat 
Evoked Potentials (CHEPs), Cold Evoked Potentials 
(CEPs), and Quantitative Sensory Testing (QST). CHEPs 
were obtained by delivering fifteen heat stimuli of 55 °C 
at 30–45s intervals, while CEPs consisted of fifteen cold 
stimuli of 9  °C applied at similar intervals. QST was 
performed to determine thresholds for warm and cold 
detection as well as for heat and cold pain perception, 
providing a detailed quantitative evaluation of small sen­
sory fiber integrity.
Neuropsychological and neuropsychiatric evaluation
Neuropsychological and neuropsychiatric assessments 
were administered by an experienced neuropsycholo­
gist. Demographic and clinical variables were recorded 
for all participants, and overall cognitive function was 
screened using the Montreal Cognitive Assessment 
(MoCA) [37, 38]. A comprehensive cognitive battery was 
subsequently performed to evaluate attention, execu­
tive function, memory, language, and perceptual abili­
ties. Attention and processing speed were assessed with 
the Trail Making Test A (TMT-A) [39] and the Salthouse 
Perceptual Comparison Test (SPCT) [40, 41]; execu­
tive function and cognitive flexibility were measured 
through the Trail Making Test B (TMT-B), the Modified 
Wisconsin Card Sorting Test (M-WCST) [42, 43], and 
the Stroop Test [44]; verbal fluency was evaluated using 
both semantic (animals) and phonemic (P) categories; 
memory was examined with the Hopkins Verbal Learn­
ing Test–Revised (HVLT-R) [45]; and perceptual abilities 
were assessed with the Benton Judgment of Line Orienta­
tion (JLO) [46]. Sustained attention was evaluated using 
the Toulouse-Piéron Revised (TP-R) test [47]. Neuropsy­
chiatric status was further characterized with validated 
questionnaires addressing fatigue (Modified Fatigue 
Impact Scale, MFIS) [48], depressive symptoms (Geri­
atric Depression Scale, GDS) [49], anxiety (State-Trait 
Anxiety Inventory, STAI) [50], suicidal ideation (Colum­
bia Suicide Severity Rating Scale, C-SSRS) [51], and sleep 
quality (Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index, PSQI) [52].
Statistical analysis
Statistical analyses were performed using IBM SPSS Sta­
tistics 26.0 and R (version 4.4.2). Descriptive statistics 
were used to summarize demographic and clinical char­
acteristics, expressed as median and interquartile range 
(IQR) for continuous variables and counts and percent­
ages for categorical variables. Because several variables 
showed non-normal distributions and the sample size 
was relatively small, non-parametric approaches were 
applied.
Eighteen domain-level composite scores were cre­
ated to summarize the multidomain assessment and 
reduce the number of individual outcome measures. The

Median disease duration at baseline was 12 months 
(IQR = 10.5–24.5) for PCC and 42 months (IQR = 32–100) 
for ME/CFS. The median interval between evaluations 
was comparable (PCC: 30 months, IQR = 24.5–36.5; ME/

Baseline domain-level comparisons
At baseline, ME/CFS and PCC showed largely overlap­
ping profiles across sensory, autonomic, cognitive, and 
clinical symptom domains (Table  1). However, nomi­
nal between-group differences emerged in some physi­
ological measures. ME/CFS patients exhibited higher 
QST warm detection thresholds compared with PCC 
(median [IQR]: 0.62 [− 0.25–0.81] vs. − 0.11 [− 0.72–0.52]; 
p = 0.034), indicating greater thermal sensory dysfunc­
tion. In the autonomic domain, ME/CFS showed a higher 
burden of autonomic symptoms as assessed by COM­
PASS-31 (0.60 [− 0.33–1.10] vs. − 0.27 [− 1.09–0.21]; 
p = 0.038), alongside lower hemodynamic composite 
scores compared with PCC (− 0.14 [− 0.35–0.02] vs. 
0.18 [− 0.25–0.53]; p = 0.019). None of these differences 
remained significant after FDR correction. No significant 
between-group differences were observed across cogni­
tive domains or clinical symptom measures at baseline.
Multidimensional correlation patterns
Significant cross–domain associations emerged between 
cognitive, sensory, autonomic, and symptom domains 
at baseline (Fig.  1). Cognitive domains showed strong 
positive intercorrelations, indicating internal consis­
tency across neuropsychological composite scores. 
Markers of peripheral sensory and autonomic dysfunc­
tion were significantly related to symptom burden. Small 
fiber symptom severity was positively associated with 
autonomic symptom burden (Rho = 0.65, pFDR = 0.002) 
and with fatigue severity (Rho = 0.55, pFDR = 0.018). 
Fatigue showed consistent negative associations with 
multiple cognitive domains, including processing speed 
(Rho = − 0.57, pFDR = 0.004), attention (Rho = − 0.49, 
pFDR = 0.018), and executive function (Rho = − 0.44, 
pFDR = 0.047), suggesting that greater fatigue burden was 
associated with lower cognitive performance.
A moderate association was also observed between 
autonomic symptom burden and fatigue (Rho = 0.47, 
p = 0.007, pFDR = 0.050), although this result was bor­
derline after FDR correction. Associations involving psy­
chiatric symptoms and evoked potential measures were 
weaker and did not survive FDR correction.
Longitudinal changes from baseline to follow-up
At follow-up, no significant differences between-group 
were observed across sensory/small-fiber, autonomic, 
domains included Autonomic Symptoms, Hemodynamic 
function, Sympathetic function, Parasympathetic func­
tion, Small Fiber Symptoms, CHEPs, CEPs, QST Warm, 
QST Cold, Fatigue, Psychiatric symptoms, Fluency, Pro­
cessing Speed, Attention, Verbal Memory, Executive 
Function, Visuospatial function, and Global Cognition. 
Analyses were conducted at the domain level; therefore, 
the multidomain approach refers to the parallel assess­
ment of complementary autonomic, small-fiber/sensory, 
cognitive, and clinical symptom domains rather than for­
mal multimodal statistical integration.
Cross-domain associations at baseline were assessed 
using Spearman rank correlation coefficients, and 
within-subject change from baseline to follow-up using 
Wilcoxon signed-rank tests. Repeated-measures effects 
were examined using rank-transformed linear mixed-
effects models fitted separately for each domain, with 
subject-level random intercepts and Time (baseline 
vs. follow-up) as the primary fixed effect. Additional 
models tested Time × Group and Time × disease dura­
tion effects. To assess the potential influence of baseline 
disease-duration imbalance, sensitivity models testing 
Time × Group were repeated including baseline disease 
duration and the Time × disease duration interaction. In 
addition, complete covariate-adjusted rank-transformed 
mixed-effects models were fitted across the 18 domains, 
including Time, Group, Time × Group, baseline disease 
duration, Time × disease duration, age, education level, 
interval between assessments, and subject-level random 
intercepts. Full model outputs are reported in Supple­
mentary Tables 1–5.
To examine whether baseline domain status predicted 
two-timepoint follow-up change, median quantile regres­
sion models were fitted with baseline values as predictors 
and longitudinal change (follow-up minus baseline) as 
the outcome. This analysis was used to explore baseline-
dependent effects in domains showing FDR-significant 
Time effects in the fully adjusted models.
A two-tailed significance threshold of p < 0.05 was 
applied throughout, and false discovery rate (FDR) cor­
rection was applied within analytic families. FDR cor­
rection was performed using the Benjamini–Hochberg 
procedure, applied separately within each family of 
effects across the 18 prespecified domains. FDR-adjusted 
p-values (pFDR) < 0.05 were considered statistically 
significant.

17 with ME/CFS. The PCC group comprised 17 women 
(81%), with a median age of 51 years (IQR = 47.5–57.0), 
and the ME/CFS group included 16 women (94.1%), with 
a median age of 43 years (IQR = 36.5–50.0).

Discussion
Building on the increasing recognition that PCC and ME/
CFS may represent points along a shared post-infectious 
spectrum, the present study was designed to character­
ize their physiological changes from baseline to follow-
up using a comprehensive multidomain assessment. By 
assessing autonomic function, small-fiber integrity, clini­
cal symptoms, and neurocognitive performance at two 
time points, we were able to examine both within-sub­
ject change and cross-domain relationships across two 
assessment timepoints.
Within this framework, our findings provide evidence 
of substantial overlap between PCC and ME/CFS across 
autonomic, sensory, and cognitive domains at baseline, 
with selective differences restricted to sensory and auto­
nomic measures. ME/CFS patients exhibited greater 
thermal sensory dysfunction and higher autonomic 
symptom burden, likely reflecting longer disease dura­
tion rather than distinct mechanisms. Cognitive perfor­
mance did not differ between groups, arguing against

change is compatible with regression to the mean, 
because participants with the most impaired baseline 
responses showed the largest increases over time. There­

measurement variability, statistical regression, or a com­

incomplete sensory change, consistent with previous lon­
gitudinal small fiber neuropathy observations [56].
Autonomic changes were characterized by increased 
parasympathetic and reduced sympathetic activity over 
time, suggesting partial autonomic rebalancing rather 
than global recovery. Hemodynamic measures remained 
largely stable, consistent with cardiovascular decon­
ditioning [57]. Clinically overt orthostatic syndromes 
were uncommon, supporting the heterogeneous nature 
of autonomic dysfunction in PCC and ME/CFS [11]. 
Unadjusted models suggested possible Time × Group 
effects in hemodynamic function and autonomic symp­
tom burden, but these effects did not remain significant 
after adjustment for baseline disease duration. Therefore, 
evidence for group-specific autonomic change should be 
interpreted cautiously.
Cognitive changes were modest and selective. Verbal 
memory showed a small but significant improvement 
over time, whereas processing speed showed a signifi­
cant decrease in the rank-based longitudinal models, 
while global cognition, executive function, attention, 
and verbal fluency remained largely stable [58, 59]. The 
improvement in verbal memory should also be inter­
preted cautiously, as repeated neuropsychological testing

supports a model of relative cognitive stability with lim­
ited domain-specific change. Importantly, no cognitive 
domain exhibited robust FDR-corrected evidence of dif­
ferential change between PCC and ME/CFS after adjust­
ment for disease duration, further reinforcing broadly 
overlapping patterns of change.
Despite objective changes in several physiological 
domains, subjective symptom measures showed limited 
parallel improvement. This dissociation between physi­
ological markers and perceived symptom burden likely 
reflects complex interactions between chronic illness, 
central sensory processing, fatigue, and illness percep­
tion. Persistent symptom severity in the context of partial 
physiological change has been reported in other chronic 
post-infectious conditions and may be reinforced by 
long-standing maladaptive fatigue–pain–sleep cycles and 
heightened interoceptive awareness [60].
From a clinical perspective, these findings highlight the 
value of comprehensive multidomain assessment in PCC 
and ME/CFS. The observed domain-specific changes 
suggest that targeted interventions such as autonomic 
rehabilitation and symptom-oriented management may 
leverage residual physiological reserve. At the same 
time, the persistence of subjective burden despite objec­
tive changes underscores the need for approaches that 
address central symptom integration, illness perception, 
and long-term coping strategies [60, 61].
Several limitations warrant consideration. The modest 
sample size, particularly given the multidomain nature 
of the study and the number of outcomes assessed, lim­
its statistical power to detect subtle group-specific effects 
and increases the risk of sample-specific findings. The 
two-timepoint follow-up design also precludes char­
acterization of dynamic trajectories, and the absence of 
a healthy longitudinal control group limits the ability 
to distinguish true biological change from compensa­
tory adaptation, test-retest variability, practice effects, 
or regression to the mean. Baseline disease duration also 
differed substantially between groups and may contribute 
to variability in follow-up change. Although duration-
adjusted sensitivity analyses did not show FDR-signif­
icant Time × Group effects or consistent modulation of 
change by disease duration, residual confounding cannot 
be excluded. Accordingly, the absence of robust group-
specific effects should not be interpreted as definitive 
evidence of disease convergence or identical follow-up 
patterns. Rather, these findings support broadly overlap­
ping multidomain profiles within the limits of the avail­
able sample and should be replicated in larger cohorts 
with additional follow-up timepoints and appropriate 
control groups.
Conclusions
In summary, this two-timepoint follow-up study sup­
ports substantial overlap between PCC and ME/CFS 
across autonomic, small-fiber/sensory, cognitive, and 
clinical symptom domains. Among individuals with 
persistent symptoms, PCC may come to resemble lon­
ger-standing ME/CFS across several physiological and 
cognitive domains.
