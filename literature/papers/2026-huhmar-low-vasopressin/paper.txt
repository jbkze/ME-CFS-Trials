Objective: The complex clinical picture of myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/chronic fatigue syndrome 
(CFS) includes orthostatic intolerance with symptoms and signs suggesting abnormal water ho-
meostasis and hypovolemia. Since many ME/CFS patients report polyuria-polydipsia, we conducted 
an observational study based on series of plasma and urine osmolality (P-Osm and U-Osm) as well 
as plasma levels of vasopressin (P-VP) or copeptin in consecutive patients diagnosed with ME/CFS 
according to the International Consensus Criteria.
Methods: P-VP as well as P-Osm and U-Osm were measured in 111 patients after 10 h overnight 
fasting and fluid deprivation. Additional 13 patients were assessed for copeptin, when P-VP mea-
surements were no longer available. The clinical routine also included brain MRI and blood 
chemistry.
Results: P-Osm was abnormally high (>292 mOsm/kg) in 71 of 124 (57.3%) and U-Osm below the 
reference interval (<750 mOsm/kg) in 82 of 124 (66.1%) patients. P-VP was below the level of 
detection (<1.6 pg/mL) in 91 of 111 (82.0%) patients. A normal P-VP level compared with their 
P-Osm was found in 11 of 111 (9.9%) patients. Copeptin levels were all within the given reference 
range, albeit in the lower end in most patients. No indication of relevant pathology in either 
hypothalamus or hypophysis was present.
Conclusions: Our findings suggest that chronic down-regulation of VP mimicking central diabetes 
insipidus is an important measurable part of the disease mechanism that potentially contributes to 
criterial symptoms of ME/CFS.

Introduction
Myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome (ME/CFS) 
is an illness characterized by intolerance to upright posture and 
exertion. Even low intensity physical and/or cognitive exertion 
triggers postmalaise, long-lasting or persistent impairment of 
symptoms including pain, sleep disturbance, neurological, auto-
nomic, gastrointestinal and/or immunologic symptoms (Table 1).
ME/CFS is not a rare condition, with prevalence estimation of 0.89% 
of the population. 1 Still, its pathophysiology remains elusive.
Vasopressin (VP), also called antidiuretic hormone, has a central 
role in the regulation of water balance and maintenance of both suf-
ficient fluid volume and blood pressure, which are prerequisites of 
orthostatic tolerance. 2 VP synthesized by neurons in the hypothala-
mus is secreted from the posterior lobe of the hypophysis as a 
response to increased plasma osmolality. The hypothalamic osmor-
eceptors are very sensitive: only 1% rise in plasma osmolarity triggers 
VP secretion, and VP exerts an antidiuretic effect in the kidneys via 
V2-receptors leading to water retention and normalizing plasma 
osmolality. 3,4 Through V1 receptors expressed in blood vessels, VP 
facilitates vasoconstriction. 5 Finally, VP acts in concert with adrenergic 
neural activity in locus coeruleus, with potential implications for 
ME/CFS symptomatology, as central noradrenergic activity rises when 
upright, maintaining a tonic stimulation of vasopressin secretion. 6-8

Hypovolemia is described among patients with ME/CFS. 9-11 Pa-
tients with ME/CFS often describe excessive thirst and high urine 
volumes, symptoms that are regularly dismissed if not specifically 
asked about. We report a clinical observational study on a series of 
osmolality and VP/copeptin levels in 124 patients with ME/CFS and 
discuss the potential role of disturbed VP function in ME/CFS 
symptomatology.
Materials and Methods
This retrospective cross-sectional study of consecutive patients
with ME/CFS was carried out at The Bragee ME-center in Stock-
holm, Sweden. Between March 2019 and November 2020, our 
neurologist (H.H.) met 173 consecutive patients with ME/CFS 
diagnosed according to the International Consensus Criteria. 12 
Thirty-two patients were ineligible due to i) diabetes mellitus 
(n = 5); ii) severe rheumatoid arthritis (n = 1); iii) coming from 
outside the Stockholm region (n = 6); iv) inability to undertake 
overnight water deprivation and/or holding urine (n = 10);
v) inability to reach a laboratory within the time-frame (n = 6); or
vi) miscellaneous reasons (n = 4).
As standard clinical routine, all 141 recruitable patients 
delivered morning samples of plasma VP (P-VP), plasma osmo-
lality (P-Osm) and urine osmolality (U-Osm) after 10-hour water 
deprivation and fasting. In 12 patients, the results were incomplete 
due to technical problems or the patient’s inability to hold urine 
overnight. In the last 13 patients, copeptin measurement was 
delivered instead of VP, as RIA measurements were in late 2020 
withdrawn to minimize the use of radioactive reactants, and 
copeptin mirrors the level of VP. 13 Thus, out of the laboratory 
requests for 141 patients, reliable measurements were completed 
in 124 patients (111 with P-VP, and 13 with P-copeptin).
All laboratory assessments were done by an accredited labo-
ratory (osmolalities and VP at Karolinska University Hospital,
copeptin at Link€oping University Hospital). Assessment of osmo-
lality was done according to classical freeze/thawing procedures. 
VP was analyzed by a radioimmunoassay with an intra-assay co-
efficient of variation less than 4% and an ability to detect vaso-
pressin at the level of 1.6 pg/ml. Copeptin was measured 
immunochemically with a ThermoFisher (BRAHMS Kryptor Gold) 
instrument that bases on the TRACE-technics (Time-Resolved 
Amplified Cryptate Emission). The reported measurement error is 
about 6% to 9% with low levels (around 5 pmol/L) and about 5% at 
high levels (95 pmol/L).
The standard diagnostic procedure included brain MRI and 
measurements of plasma glucose, sodium, and potassium, as well 
as serum thyroid stimulating hormone and cortisol.
The demographic data of the 124 study participants are shown 
in Table 2. Sex distribution, age ranges and body mass indices were 
typical of patients with ME/CFS. The average/median disease 
duration was 7.3 or 7.5 years. None of the participants were taking 
desmopressin or lithium.
Ethical approval was granted by the Swedish Ethical Review 
Authority (nr. 2021-03114).
Results
Of all 124 patients, P-Osm was high (>292 mOsm/kg) in 71 
(57.3%) and U-Osm low (<750 mOsm/kg) in 82 (66.1%) patients.
The combined results of P-VP, P-Osm, and U-Osm of the 111 VP-
patients are presented in Fig. 1. P-VP levels remained very low in 
most patients, with levels under detection threshold (1.6 pg/ml) in 
86 out of 111 (77.5%). Urine osmolality was low (<750 mOsm/kg) in 
74 out of 111 (66.7%). Eleven patients (9.9%) had a normal level of
P-VP in relation to their P-Osm, and 5 of them (4.5%) were within 
the reference range for all the 3 parameters. Finally, 57 individuals 
had elevated P-Osm with simultaneous pathologically low levels of 
P-VP, ie lower-right area of ‘central diabetes insipidus’ in Fig. 1.
Copeptin levels were all within the given reference range, albeit 
in the lower end in most patients (Figs. 2 and 3).
Blood chemistry including sodium, potassium, glucose, TSH 
and cortisol levels were within normal range. MRI scanning was 
performed in 111 patients with VP measurements. In 2 additional 
cases, MRI was either not obtained or not available. Among the 
completed scans, 3 showed an empty sella, while the remaining 
studies were considered normal. In the group of 13 patients with 
copeptin measurements, one had a 2-mm pituitary (adenohy-
pophyseal) cyst, one scan could not be obtained, and the rest were 
normal. Accordingly, the frequency of abnormal hypophyseal MR 
findings among the scans was 3.6%; none showed laboratory 
findings consistent with panhypopituitarism.
There was no correlation between P- and U-Osm (Fig. 4) 
depicting the failure of rising P-Osm to stimulate excretion of VP in 
this patient cohort. Fourteen individuals had both P-Osm and U-
Osm within the reference range (Fig. 4, shaded area).
The mean copeptin level in all subjects was 7.6 pmol/l (SEM 
1.49). More detailed results in both genders are reported in Table 2. 
Copeptin over plasma osmolality was within reference range in all 
the 13 patients, with a positive correlation with U-Osm 
(R 2 = 0.619, Fig. 4). Copeptin 7.5 pmol/l, which is within the 
reference range for copeptin, turned out to be a cut-off level dis-
tinguishing those patients with abnormally low U-Osm from those 
with normal U-Osm.
Discussion
Our results reveal a high prevalence of low P-VP, low urine 
osmolality and high plasma osmolality after overnight water re-
striction. These findings are in line with polydipsia-polyuria, 
which is a common complaint when specifically asked about 
during the clinical interview. Polydipsia-polyuria often appears at 
the beginning of illness, and the manifestations may be constant

Clinical Relevance
Myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome is a 
prevalent, chronic and highly debilitating syndrome often 
affecting working-age individuals, adolescents, and even chil-
dren. There are no reliable biological markers for the disease 
and no proven evidence-based therapies. Further elucidation 
of the pathophysiological role of vasopressin could help clarify 
its diagnostic and therapeutic value.

often overlooked in the patient-physician communication. The 
combination of high P-Osm with low P-VP excludes both psycho-
genic polydipsia and renal resistance to VP. Instead, it indicates a 
central VP deficiency resembling central diabetes insipidus (cDI), a 
rare condition with an estimated prevalence of 1:25 000, which is 
less than one percent of the estimated prevalence of ME/CFS. 14 In 
general, cDI is mainly the result of destruction of the producing 
hypothalamic or secreting hypophyseal cells. In absence of struc-
tural abnormalities causing such a condition, cDI is considered 
idiopathic. The high incidence of central-type of VP deficiency in 
our patient cohort suggests that diminishing of the tonic stimu-
lation of VP secretion, which in a healthy person is maintained by 
central noradrenergic activity, may diminish the sensitivity of the 
osmoreceptors and cause low VP secretion in ME/CFS.
Low VP in chronic fatigue is not a novel finding. As early as in 
1993 Bakheit et al reported a low VP despite high P-Osm in 9 pa-
tients with postviral fatigue. 15 Wyller et al had similar findings in 
65 adolescents with ME/CFS. 16 A small controlled study using 
essentially the same diagnostic criteria but with a considerably 
younger patient population and a treatment arm found not only 
significantly lower levels of antidiuretic hormone (VP) and renin
activity but also consistently lower cardiac parameters and stroke 
volume index. 17 Our study of 124 consecutive ME/CFS patients 
corroborates the observation of significantly lower VP levels of 
these previous reports, which may not have been given due 
consideration over the years.
VP is of special interest in ME/CFS due to its contribution to 
orthostatic tolerance by several parallel mechanisms. Firstly, VP is 
essential for sufficient blood circulation as it maintains blood 
volume by stimulating water reabsorption in renal distal tubules, 
and it creates peripheral resistance by constricting peripheral 
blood vessels. A moderate decrease in blood volume (5% to 10%) 
causes a significant increase in VP levels primarily through 
baroreceptor reflex. 18 Thus, VP deficiency gives rise to simulta-
neous absolute hypovolemia and pooling of blood to lower ex-
tremities in upright position. This pathology predisposes any 
patient to a compensatory postural tachycardia syndrome, 
postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome, one of the few 
objective findings in ME/CFS. 19,20 Secondly, VP also acts as a 
neurotransmitter in close interaction with locus coeruleus and 
the adrenergic neural network of dorsal pontine area, which are 
of great importance for maintaining vigilance and muscle tone. 
Indeed, according to Pompeiano 6 's classical experiments, VP has a 
role in controlling posture and the gain of vestibulospinal reflex. 
Thus, in addition to the circulatory adjustments, the neurotrans-
mitter VP supports vigilance and limb extensor muscular tone 
opposing the gravitational pull, reinforcing orthostatic tolerance 
in the awake state.
VP is also involved in several other processes relevant in 
ME/CFS. As a hormone, it enhances the release of adrenocortico-
tropic hormone in both instances secretion. 21,22 Unexplained hy-
pothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis hypoactivity has been described 
in patients with ME/CFS, and acute adrenocorticotropic hormone 
in both instances receptor agonism has according to Pereira et al 
resulted in sustained symptom improvement in ME/CFS. 23-25 
Furthermore, VP is engaged in maintaining the diurnal rhythm. 26 
Indeed, sleep dysfunction, including unrefreshing sleep, inade-
quate sleep, or sleep rhythm disturbances, is obligatory in diag-
nosing ME/CFS according to the International criteria. 
Furthermore, VP is also involved in energy metabolism as pe-
ripheral enzymatic degradation of VP is linked with glucose intake 
in adipose and muscle cells. 27 Consequently, low VP levels may 
have a bearing on energy metabolism of musculature. 28

Chronic hyperosmolality ensuing from recurrent or sustained 
down-regulation of VP secretion has adverse effects on several 
functions. Hyperosmolality attenuates post-exercise sweating, 
providing a reasonable explanation for the elevated body tem-
perature or sensation of fever typically reported by ME/CFS pa-
tients after exertion. 29 Elevated tissue temperature combined with 
hypovolemia is detrimental to aerobic performance. 30 On cellular
level, the compensatory mechanisms may be altered by hyper-
osmolar extracellular fluid: Esfandyarpour and colleagues have 
shown that exposing peripheral blood mononuclear cells of 
ME/CFS patients to hyperosmotic stress reveals an impedance 
modulation response profoundly different from that of healthy 
controls. 31 Furthermore, hyperosmolality may even contribute to 
sensitization of neurons, an essential feature in ME/CFS

The limitation of the study is the cross-sectional, observational 
nature of the series as an aside of clinical work, precluding the use 
of recruited healthy controls. An additional limitation is the extent 
of missing data; 10 patients who were able to visit the ME-center 
were unable to even undertake the regimen with overnight fluid 
deprivation and holding of urine, and further 17 patients were not 
able to fully comply with it. In addition, we obviously could not 
recruit the most severely affected, completely bedbound patients 
not tolerating upright position at all. On the other hand, this il-
lustrates the debilitating nature of both polydipsia-polyuria and 
orthostatic intolerance in ME/CFS, and the dropouts may be ex-
pected to represent the severe end of the spectrum. A pragmatic 
limitation in the future may be the availability of VP measure-
ments. Copeptin is secreted in equimolar amount to VP, and whilst 
VP has a short half-life (5-20 min), copeptin has shown to be a 
more stable molecule reflecting long-term VP levels. 35 Thus, it 
might not be as sensitive to abnormal dynamic changes of VP 
secretion. Due to the small size of our copeptin subpopulation, it is 
not possible to conclude whether copeptin measurements are 
equally informative in the diagnostics of this condition.
The strengths are the large consecutive cohort of carefully 
diagnosed ME/CFS patients, as well as the relative consistency of 
the findings. A further strength is that we were able to measure 
vasopressin levels directly in most patients, rather than relying on 
copeptin.
Our findings underscore the need for careful history taking in 
clinical practice, where polydipsia-polyuria provide further sup-
port for the diagnosis after exclusion of diabetes mellitus and 
related differential diagnoses. Further study should be aimed at 
determining the diagnostic value of low U-osm after 10-hour 
overnight water deprivation, if necessary in combination with 
copeptin/VP/P-Osm, in screening or even as a biomarker for a ME/ 
CFS subpopulation.
The high incidence of low VP in our patient cohort suggests that 
chronic down-regulation of VP secretion may be an elementary 
part of pathophysiology of ME/CFS, first and foremost the chronic 
hypovolemia which hitherto has remained unexplained. Finally, 
understanding the underlying pathophysiology will be essential, 
including the role of autoimmunity as suggested by a previous 
case-control study on antipituitary and antihypothalamic anti-
bodies. 36 Given the multitude of the functions of VP as a hormone 
and a neurotransmitter, a chronic VP deficiency could be the 
reason for a wide array of the criteria symptoms of ME/CFS. Ulti-
mately, a deeper understanding of this pathophysiology has the 
potential to inform more effective therapeutic strategies for the 
patients affected.
